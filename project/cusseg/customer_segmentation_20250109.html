1. Background¶

In today’s competitive market, understanding customer behavior is essential for businesses to optimize their marketing strategies, product offerings, and customer engagement efforts. With the rise of e-commerce and omnichannel retailing, consumers interact with brands across multiple touchpoints, leading to diverse purchasing behaviors and preferences. Identifying distinct customer segments allows businesses to tailor their approaches, ensuring personalized experiences that drive higher engagement, conversion rates, and long-term loyalty.

Customer segmentation is a powerful tool that enables companies to group customers based on demographics, shopping behaviors, and spending patterns. By leveraging machine learning techniques such as K-Means clustering, businesses can analyze vast amounts of data to uncover hidden patterns and gain actionable insights into consumer habits. This data-driven approach helps in designing effective promotions, pricing strategies, and personalized recommendations, ultimately leading to enhanced customer satisfaction and business growth.

2. Problem Statement¶

Despite the availability of vast consumer data, many businesses struggle to effectively segment their customers and implement targeted marketing strategies. Traditional segmentation methods often rely on basic demographics and fail to capture complex behavioral traits such as purchase frequency, spending habits, and product preferences. This limitation results in generic marketing campaigns that fail to resonate with specific customer groups, leading to missed opportunities for engagement and revenue growth.

To address this challenge, this portfolio applies K-Means clustering to categorize customers into distinct segments based on their shopping behavior, spending patterns, and preferred product categories. By analyzing these clusters, businesses can develop personalized marketing campaigns, loyalty programs, and promotional strategies that better align with customer needs. This segmentation approach aims to bridge the gap between data analytics and strategic decision-making, ensuring a more customer-centric business model that enhances both consumer experience and profitability.

3. Variables¶

This dataset contains 2000 synthetic customer records with 10 key attributes that describe customer demographics, financial metrics, purchasing behavior, and product preferences. It is not real-life data but has been generated for demonstration purposes in customer segmentation analysis.

The dataset is designed to facilitate the study of customer behavior patterns and to help identify distinct groups of customers based on their spending habits, income levels, and shopping preferences.

  1. Customer Demographics
  • Customer_ID (int): A unique identifier assigned to each customer.
  • Age (int): The age of the customer, ranging from 22 to 58 years with an average of 39.39 years.
  • Gender (object): The gender of the customer, categorized as Male or Female.
  1. Financial Attributes
  • Annual_Income (int): The annual earnings of the customer in currency units, ranging from 49,180 to 116,601, with a mean income of 85,417.
  • Spending_Score (int): A metric measuring the customer's spending behavior, ranging from 10 to 99, with a median value of 60. Higher values indicate higher spending tendencies.
  1. Subscription & Engagement Behavior
  • Subscription_Type (object): The type of subscription plan the customer has chosen, categorized as Basic, Standard, or Premium.
  • Purchase_Frequency (int): The number of purchases made within a specific time frame, ranging from 1 to 29 purchases, with a median value of 10.
  • Customer_Tenure (int): The number of years the customer has been active, ranging from 1 to 9 years, with an average tenure of 4.66 years.
  1. Purchasing Channels & Preferences
  • Channel (object): The primary shopping medium used by the customer, categorized as Online, In-Store, or Mobile App.
  • Product_Category (object): The preferred product category of the customer, including Electronics, Clothing, Automotive, Beauty, Sports, Toys, Books, and Home & Kitchen.

  • Each numerical variable was centered to have a mean of 0.
  • The values were scaled to have a unit variance.
  • This ensures that all features contribute equally to the clustering process.

The first principal component (PC1) captures the contrast between customer maturity and engagement patterns, showing strong positive correlations with Age (0.560) and Customer_Tenure (0.522), while exhibiting negative correlations with Spending_Score (-0.542) and Purchase_Frequency (-0.346). This component effectively distinguishes between older, established customers (positive values) and younger, more active customers (negative values). The second principal component (PC2) primarily represents customer purchasing power, with a dominant positive correlation with Annual_Income (0.878) and a moderate positive correlation with Purchase_Frequency (0.440). Higher positive values in PC2 indicate customers with greater income and purchasing frequency.

These transformed values effectively capture the essential characteristics of customers while simplifying the data structure.

The reduced dimensions provide a strong foundation for subsequent clustering analysis, enabling clear differentiation between customer segments. The separation along PC1 allows for distinction between mature, established customers and younger, active shoppers, while PC2 distribution helps identify income-based segments within these age/activity groups. 5. K-Means Clustering¶

5.1. Sturges' Rule¶

The K-Means clustering algorithm was applied to segment customers based on their demographic, financial, and behavioral attributes. To determine the most suitable number of clusters, a combination of Sturges' Rule, the Elbow Method, and Silhouette Analysis was utilized.

A widely used heuristic for estimating the optimal number of clusters (K) is Sturges' Rule, which suggests: $$ K = 1 + 3.322 \log_{10}(N) $$ where $N$ is the total number of data points. Given that this dataset contains 2000 customer records, the estimated number of clusters is: $$ K = 1 + 3.322 \log_{10}(2000) \approx 12 $$ Sturges' Rule is chosen for several key reasons:

  1. Balanced Scalability:
  • Provides reasonable numbers across all dataset sizes
  • Logarithmic growth prevents over-segmentation in large datasets
  • Maintains meaningful clusters even with small datasets
  1. Business Practicality:
  • Produces manageable and interpretable cluster numbers
  • Facilitates actionable business strategies
  • Balances analytical precision with practical implementation

This approach helps balance the trade-off between granularity and practicality in customer segmentation, ensuring that the resulting clusters remain meaningful and actionable from a business perspective.

5.2. Elbow Method¶

The Elbow Method is a widely used technique for selecting the optimal number of clusters in K-Means clustering. It evaluates the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS), which measures how compact and well-separated clusters are. A lower WCSS indicates that data points are closer to their respective cluster centroids, suggesting well-defined clusters.

The optimal number of clusters, K, is determined by identifying the "elbow point" in the WCSS curve. This is the point where adding more clusters results in diminishing improvements in compactness, making further increases in K unnecessary.

The WCSS formula is given by:

$$ WCSS = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{j \in C_i} || x_j - \mu_i ||^2 $$

where:

  • ${K}$ is the number of clusters,
  • ${𝐶_i}$ represents each cluster,
  • ${𝑥_j}$ is a data point in cluster
  • ${𝜇_𝑖}$ is the centroid of cluster
  • ${∣∣𝑥_j-𝜇_𝑖∣^2}$ is the squared Euclidean distance between a data point and its cluster centroid.

Using KneeLocator, the elbow point was identified at K = 4, indicating that 4 clusters provide the best trade-off between cluster compactness and segmentation quality. KneeLocator is an automated method for detecting the "elbow point" in the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) curve, where adding more clusters results in diminishing improvements in compactness. This ensures that the selected number of clusters balances data segmentation quality while avoiding unnecessary complexity.

5.3. Silhouette Score¶

Silhouette Score is a metric used to evaluate the quality of clustering by measuring how well each data point fits within its assigned cluster. The Silhouette Score (S) is calculated as: $$ S = \frac{b - a}{\max(a, b)} $$

where:

  • $𝑎$ is the average intra-cluster distance (how close a point is to other points in the same cluster).
  • $𝑏$ is the average nearest-cluster distance (how far a point is from points in the closest neighboring cluster).

5.4. Silhouette Plots¶

The Silhouette Plot is used to evaluate the quality of clustering by visualizing how well each data point fits within its assigned cluster. It provides insights into:

  • Cluster separation: Measures how distinct clusters are from one another.
  • Intra-cluster cohesion: Shows how tightly grouped points are within the same cluster.
  • Misclassified points: Identifies points that may belong to a different cluster.

Each cluster’s silhouette width represents how similar its points are compared to neighboring clusters. A higher silhouette score indicates better-defined clusters, while a low or negative score suggests overlapping or poorly separated clusters.

Silhouette plots help assess whether the chosen number of clusters (K) is appropriate, guiding refinements in the clustering process.

# Silhouette Plot
def plot_silhouette(n_clusters, X):
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 4, figsize=(20, 10))
    axes = axes.flatten()
    
    for i, k in enumerate(range(2, 10)):
        kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42)
        cluster_labels = kmeans.fit_predict(X)
        silhouette_avg = silhouette_score(X, cluster_labels)
        sample_silhouette_values = silhouette_samples(X, cluster_labels)
        
        y_lower = 10
        for j in range(k):
            ith_cluster_silhouette_values = sample_silhouette_values[np.where(cluster_labels == j)]
            ith_cluster_silhouette_values.sort()
            size_cluster_j = ith_cluster_silhouette_values.shape[0]
            y_upper = y_lower + size_cluster_j
            color = cm.nipy_spectral(float(j) / k)
            axes[i].fill_betweenx(np.arange(y_lower, y_upper), 0, ith_cluster_silhouette_values, facecolor=color, edgecolor=color, alpha=0.7)
            y_lower = y_upper + 10  
        
        axes[i].set_title(f'Silhouette Plot for k={k}')
        axes[i].set_xlabel('Silhouette Coefficient Values')
        axes[i].set_ylabel('Cluster Label')
        axes[i].axvline(x=silhouette_avg, color='red', linestyle='--')
        axes[i].text(0.9, 0.02, f'Silhouette: {silhouette_avg:.2f}', transform=axes[i].transAxes, fontsize=10, verticalalignment='bottom', horizontalalignment='right')
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

5.5. Optimal K¶

The optimal number of clusters was determined by analyzing both the elbow method and silhouette analysis. The elbow method, implemented through KneeLocator, identified k=4 as the optimal point where adding more clusters yields diminishing returns in Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS). Conversely, the silhouette analysis indicated k=5 as the optimal choice, demonstrating the highest silhouette score of 0.48 and displaying well-balanced cluster characteristics.

Following the conservative principle of cluster selection,the minimum value between these two metrics (k=4) was chosen as the final optimal number of clusters. This choice ensures the model maintains strong cluster cohesion while avoiding over-segmentation, providing a practical balance between statistical validity and interpretability. This conservative approach aligns with business requirements for meaningful and actionable customer segments.

# Function to create a radar chart
def create_radar_chart(ax, values, title):
    angles = [n / float(N) * 2 * pi for n in range(N)]
    values = np.append(values, values[0])  # Close the radar chart
    angles += angles[:1]  # Close the radar chart

    ax.plot(angles, values, linewidth=2, linestyle='solid')
    ax.fill(angles, values, alpha=0.4)
    ax.set_xticks(angles[:-1])
    ax.set_xticklabels(categories, fontsize=10)
    ax.set_title(title, fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')

8. Discussion¶

The analysis reveals fundamental insights about customer behavior patterns, showing that shopping preferences are predominantly influenced by lifestyle and age rather than traditional demographic factors like gender or income. Digital engagement and channel preferences demonstrate significant variation across segments, driven by lifestyle choices rather than age alone. This is particularly evident in cross-category purchasing patterns, which offer opportunities for strategic bundling and integrated promotions. Notably, price sensitivity exists across all income levels but manifests uniquely in each segment's purchasing behavior.

Channel and service preferences emerge as critical differentiators among customer segments. A clear distinction exists between digital-first consumers and traditional retail shoppers, with multi-channel engagement patterns strongly correlating with customer lifestyle segments. These channel preferences significantly impact marketing strategy effectiveness, while digital adoption shows stronger correlation with lifestyle choices than age demographics. This understanding is crucial for developing targeted marketing approaches.

Value and service perception varies significantly across segments, with subscription tier selection serving as a strong indicator of service value perception and customer engagement level. Interestingly, the appeal of premium services doesn't directly correlate with income levels, suggesting more complex drivers of service adoption. While the importance of value proposition remains consistent across all segments, it requires different approaches to resonate effectively. Product category preferences demonstrate strong alignment with specific channel choices and service expectations.

From a marketing perspective, these findings emphasize the need for highly tailored channel strategies based on distinct segment engagement patterns. Product category focus should guide targeted promotional strategies, while cross-category bundling opportunities can be leveraged based on segment preferences. Marketing effectiveness ultimately depends on understanding and addressing segment-specific value perceptions, rather than relying on traditional demographic segmentation approaches. This nuanced understanding enables more sophisticated and effective marketing strategies that align with modern consumer behavior patterns.

9. Conclusion¶

The customer segmentation analysis reveals four distinct clusters, each with unique characteristics, preferences, and behaviors that demand tailored marketing approaches. Through comprehensive analysis of demographic, behavioral, and transactional data, clear patterns emerge that challenge traditional marketing assumptions.

The largest segment, Established Traditional Shoppers (33%), demonstrates that high income doesn't necessarily translate to premium service adoption or digital channel preference. Meanwhile, Young Digital-Savvy Spenders (21%) show that age significantly influences channel preference and service adoption patterns. The Mid-Career Digital Enthusiasts (22%) exemplify the emergence of true omnichannel shoppers, while Value-Seeking Tech Adopters (23%) prove that price sensitivity can coexist with high digital engagement and strategic shopping behavior.

Most notably, the analysis challenges conventional demographic-based marketing approaches, revealing that shopping behaviors are more strongly influenced by lifestyle choices and value perceptions than traditional demographic factors. The clear distinction between digital and traditional retail preferences, coupled with varying subscription tier adoption patterns, suggests the need for more sophisticated, channel-specific marketing strategies.

These insights provide a foundation for developing targeted marketing initiatives that align with modern consumer behavior patterns. Success in implementing these strategies will require careful consideration of channel preferences, value propositions, and service expectations specific to each segment, while maintaining consistency in overall brand positioning. By leveraging these insights, businesses can create more effective, personalized marketing approaches that resonate with each customer segment's unique characteristics and preferences.
